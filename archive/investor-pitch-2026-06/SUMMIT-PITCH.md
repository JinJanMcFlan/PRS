# Summit Pitch — VC World Summit Vancouver (June 18)

**Format:** ~90-second elevator + open-floor window. Connection generator — nobody writes a check on the floor; the real value is the follow-up.
**Raise framing:** $30K base / $250K stretch, each tied to a milestone. (Pool %/multiples are still being re-derived — do **not** quote specific percentages on the floor. Talk milestones, not terms.)
**Posture:** ~50–80% of impact is confidence, not content. Own pre-revenue as a fact and move fast to what's built. "First pitch, here to learn" is a deliberate, disarming line — not an apology.

---

## THE 90-SECOND ELEVATOR (rehearse this until it's muscle memory)

> "I'm a film and 3D guy by training — five years as a lighting artist, full production-pipeline background. For the last five months, around a full-time job, I've been building AI systems that don't just *assist* a production team — they replace whole departments. Coding, modeling, texturing — built in-house, running now.
>
> To prove they work, I use them to build real things: a video game that's already in playtesting, and in a few weeks I'm entering a global competition where winners go up on the Nasdaq billboard in Times Square. The studio is the proof. The tooling is the product — and the first one, an AI orchestrator called OCC, is what I sell.
>
> I'm pre-revenue. I built all of this solo, at night, with no budget. A small check puts me full-time right before the milestone that puts this on the map.
>
> I'm not looking for someone who needs revenue next quarter — I'm looking for someone backing what this becomes. And honestly, this is my first pitch, so I'm here to learn as much as to ask. If that's interesting, I'd love twenty minutes of your time."

**Delivery notes**
- Land the first sentence slow and certain — it sets the whole tone.
- "I'm pre-revenue" — say it flat, no flinch, then keep moving. The confidence on that line does more than the words.
- End on the question/ask, not a feature. Then stop talking and let them respond.
- Optional swap if the room is more creative than tech: lead with "a video game already in playtesting and a Times Square competition entry," then reveal the tooling underneath. Same beats, different first hook.

## STANDARD-QUESTION ANSWERS (the open-floor / follow-up conversation)

These are what investors ask *after* the 90 seconds. Know them cold; deliver conversationally, not recited.

### What does your company do? (one-liner)
> "I build AI systems that replace entire production departments — and package them so any company can do the same. Right now I'm proving it by building a whole game and film with nothing but my own pipeline. The studio's the proof; the tooling is the product."

### Stage / revenue / traction
> "Pre-revenue, by design — I've been building, not selling. In five months, solo and around a full-time job: a proprietary AI orchestration system about 75% built, a modeling-and-texturing pipeline 75–85% there, tens of thousands of lines of working code, and a game in active playtesting. The traction isn't a revenue chart yet — it's that all of this already runs."

### How much are you raising, and for what? (3 buckets)
> "Two numbers, each tied to a milestone.
> **$30K** takes me full-time immediately — clears a debt that bleeds over a thousand a month, covers six months of runway, and lets me hit the Times Square competition at first-place effort while OCC goes to launch.
> **$250K** builds the company around that — the first hires, a developer and a senior creative; the hardware and studio infrastructure; and making it a real incorporated entity with the legal and accounting done properly.
> So: **(1) go full-time, (2) build the team, (3) build the infrastructure.** The $30K buys the proof; the $250K buys the build."

### Team / why you're the right founder
> "Right now it's me — five years as a professional lighting artist, a full 3D-pipeline diploma, deep in Maya, Nuke, and now Unreal. But the real answer is what's already on disk. One person built all of this in five months around a day job. I don't wait for tools to exist — I build them. Funding doesn't change the person; it removes the constraint. There's also a partner studio, Terminal Games, that exists partly because I taught its founder AI from the ground up — that's the mission working in real life."

### Why now / why you
> "Because it already works and the timing is forcing the moment. The systems are built and running; a public proof-point — the Times Square competition — lands in weeks; and the part-time version is degrading, not holding steady, because the AI field moves every week and competing on nights and weekends means more and more manual work until I burn out. A small check turns a part-time run into a full-time one right before the milestone that makes it visible. Why me: I've already done the hard, unfunded part alone."

### What kind of investor are you looking for? (the filter — say it deliberately)
> "I'm looking for someone backing what this becomes — not someone who needs revenue next quarter. The first stretch is about building the engine, not milking it."

### The hard one — "why invest in a pre-revenue company with one month of runway?"
> "Because you're not betting on an idea, you're betting on something that already works — five months, alone, around a full-time job, built and in playtesting. Pre-revenue isn't unproven, it's early. And the runway is exactly *why now*: a small check turns a part-time run into a full-time one right before the milestone that puts it on the map. You're not funding survival — you're funding the timeline."

## How to work the floor (connection method — not a pitch)

Get curious about *them* first → "mind if I pick your brain?" → one specific small ask. For investors, let them self-select: it's a revenue share, not equity; offer 20 minutes; ask for a referral either way. For talent: "who's the most impressive [role] you know?" Always close on getting their **contact + permission to send the one-pager.** End on a question or a small ask — never on a pitch.

**Worst case is still a win:** feedback, contacts, and reps facing hard questions live. Best case is the start of a check. Walking in with that frame is why you can't lose.
