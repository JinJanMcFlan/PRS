# ProjectARC — Dad Pitch Script
**Version:** 1.0 — Pending Founder Approval
**Date:** June 9, 2026
**Pitch date:** June 13, 2026 — Las Vegas, dinner
**Purpose:** The full sit-down arc for the in-person pitch to the founder's father. This is the delivery script. The numbers and answers live in CORE-NARRATIVE.md and PLAYBOOK.md — this document is how it's *delivered*, beat by beat.

## The Audience — Read This First

Financial advisor, 35 years. Mutual funds. Selling his book of business this August. Sharp conceptual thinker. Not AI- or gaming-literate, but closer to film. Going through a divorce. Net worth ~$9M pre-divorce. Actively doing his own deal work right now. Responds to professional structure, clean numbers, and a story he can repeat to someone else.

**The single most important fact about this pitch:** He reads people across a table for money for a living. He will see a manufactured close coming from a mile away. So this script does not hide the ball. The frame is honest from the first sentence — *"I want your eyes on this, and I want you to consider coming in."* That honesty is not the soft option. With this man specifically, it is the highest-percentage play, because:

1. It keeps him in **analyst mode** — which is where he's most engaged and where his professional pride gets invested in the material holding up.
2. It removes the one thing that could blow up the relationship and the deal at once — the feeling that his own kid ran a tactic on him.
3. It lets the genuine emotional truth land **because** it doesn't feel engineered.

You are not tricking a mark. You are giving the sharpest person you know a real look at something real, and asking him to back it. Everything below serves that.

---

## The Core Principle of This Sit-Down

Most pitches try to *move* someone from skepticism to belief. With your dad, the move is different: you are **letting him stress-test it until he convinces himself**, then asking clearly. His scrutiny is the engine of the close, not the obstacle to it. Hand him the red pen on purpose. When a 35-year financial advisor picks something apart and it holds, he doesn't just fund it — he tells other people about it. That's two outcomes from one dinner.

## PHASE 0 — Setting the Dinner (before / early in the trip)

Goal: lock one dinner as the business conversation, honestly framed, without killing the fun-trip energy.

**The ask (in person or by text before the trip):**

> "Dad — one of the nights out there, I want to steal you for a proper dinner, just us. I've built something over the last five months and I'm about to start pitching it to investors. Before I put it in front of strangers, I want the sharpest financial eyes I know on it — yours. And I'll be straight with you: you're also someone I'd genuinely want involved, so I'm not just asking for notes. I'm asking you to look at it the way you'd look at any deal that crossed your desk. Deal?"

Why this works: it's flattering, it's honest, it sets the dual frame (feedback **and** a real ask), and it gives him a job he's good at. He walks in already in advisor posture — which is exactly where you want him.

**Pick the right night.** Not the first night (everyone's amped), not after the late hockey game (tired). Mid-trip, earlier dinner, decent restaurant, low enough noise to talk numbers. Bring the printed deck, the tablet, and the document — but they stay in the bag until Phase 4.

## PHASE 1 — Opening the Conversation (first ~10 minutes at the table)

Goal: set the frame, establish the real scarcity once, and get him leaning in.

**Open warm, then get to it:**

> "Okay — I'm going to actually walk you through this like you're an investor, because that's what I need from you tonight. Poke holes. If it's weak, I want to know now, before I'm sitting across from someone who isn't on my side. Sound good?"

**Plant the genuine scarcity early — once, then drop it (per the transcript: mention urgency early, lightly, then return to it later):**

> "Two quick things up front so they're not a surprise later. One — the way I've structured this, I can only bring on a small number of revenue-share investors before the percentages stop making sense for the company. That's a real ceiling, not a sales line — I'll show you the math. Two — I've already got someone committing this week, so this isn't theoretical. I'm telling you that because I'd rather you be early than find out later the room filled up."

> *(Only say the "someone committing this week" line if it is true by the time you sit down. If the pre-Friday meeting hasn't closed, cut it. Never fake this one — he'll ask who, and a fabricated answer detonates everything.)*

**The pullback (lowers pressure, contains a question — this is the technique from the transcript):**

> "And look — if backing this would put you in a tight spot with everything else you've got going on right now, I'd tell you straight up not to do it. That's not what tonight's about. Is your situation in a place where something like this is even on the table right now?"

His answer here is information you use later. If he says "yeah, I've got room" — that's banked. If he says "money's complicated right now" — you pivot to the smaller tiers and the feedback value, and you don't push the $30K. Listen to the answer.

## PHASE 2 — The Pitch (the core, ~15–20 minutes)

Deliver the CORE-NARRATIVE in spoken form. Don't read it. Hit these beats in order. Hand him the printed deck so he can annotate while you talk — annotating *is* engagement.

**Beat 1 — The real thesis (lead with the big idea, not the game):**
> "Most AI companies build tools that help people work a little faster. I'm building systems that replace entire production departments. Not assist them — replace them. I've already done it with the coding department; it's how I built everything you're about to see. The modeling and texturing department is about 90% there. That's the company. Deuterium3D — the film and game side — exists to *prove the tools work* in public, under real conditions."

**Beat 2 — Proof it's real (this is your credibility spine):**
> "And this isn't a pitch for something I'm going to build. In five months, around a full-time job, with no team and no budget, I've already built it: tens of thousands of lines of working code, a 75%-complete orchestration system, a near-complete asset pipeline, and a game that's already in playtesting. I'm not asking you to believe a plan. I'm asking you to look at what already exists."

**Beat 3 — The product that makes money first (OCC):**
> "The first thing I sell is OCC. Right now a serious AI user juggles ten different tools, copies work between them, and burns money on overlapping subscriptions — and the best tool for any job changes every month. OCC is one interface that routes everything automatically, manages the costs, and updates itself as the field shifts. For heavy users it cuts AI operating costs 20–40%. The people I'm selling to already pay for all these tools — I'm consolidating what they're already spending."

**Beat 4 — The game and why the playtest matters (use the diverse-tester detail — it's a credibility weapon):**
> "The game is Ringo. We ran a first playtest with five testers — and this matters — they weren't my buddies. US, Canada, and England, men and women, a deliberate spread of skill levels. The guy in England I'd only met in March. He played three hours straight in a rough greybox build because the movement felt that good. Four of the five said, unprompted, they'd never seen anything like it. One quit after five minutes because it was too hard for him — and that one's the most useful result I got, because it confirmed the design problem I already knew about: the game has to *teach* through how the levels are built, not through tutorials. That tester's coming back for the next round specifically to see if it teaches him. He's not annoyed — he's curious."

**Beat 5 — The funding sequence (make the timeline feel inevitable):**
> "Here's why the timing is real. There's a competition — TRC — due July 15. Winners go on the Nasdaq billboard in Times Square. That entry is built entirely with my AI pipeline, so placing in it is third-party proof the tools work. That proof feeds an Epic Games grant — up to $150K, no equity, no repayment — due in early September. So the sequence is: your capital → I go full-time → TRC → billboard → a credible track record → maximum grant. Your money is what makes that whole chain possible on this timeline."

**Beat 6 — What full-time actually buys (concrete, not abstract):**
> "Right now, working around a job, I can hit maybe top 20 in that competition on 60–70 hour weeks split between the two. Full-time, that same 60 hours goes entirely into the competition — and I'm going for *first*, not just placing. That's the difference funding makes. It doesn't give me the ability to work. It removes the ceiling on it."

## PHASE 3 — Letting Him Probe (this is where the deal is won)

He's a financial advisor. He *will* go after the numbers. Welcome it. Every answer you have ready makes the next objection smaller. Pull the full answers from PLAYBOOK.md — below is how to *carry* them.

**Posture rules:**
- Never get defensive. A calm, specific answer to a hard question is the single most persuasive thing that happens all night.
- Use the pullback-with-a-question whenever pressure spikes (transcript technique): acknowledge, ask a sincere question that lowers the temperature, *then* answer.
- Close every handled objection with **"Does that sound fair for the most part?"** — not "does that make sense" (which reopens it). "Fair for the most part" assumes agreement and moves forward.
- After you answer, **loop straight back into the pitch** — never go silent after handling an objection. Silence invites a new one.

**The structure questions he'll likely hit (carry these cold):**
- *Why revenue share not equity* → you keep control, he has no governance overhead, he gets paid on performance. (He taught you this — see the anchor in Phase 4.)
- *Why gross not net* → net can be massaged, gross protects him.
- *How 4% returns 2x* → walk the $750K-to-cap math.
- *What if it earns nothing* → he's owed nothing, same as any early-stage bet, but with no dilution and no board seat.
- *Why 2x cap* → defensible, fair, designed to close not to haggle.

**On the deal numbers, use APCL when he digs in (full script in Playbook §1):**
> A *(pullback + question):* "Fair — and you're not committing to anything by asking. Is it the percentage you're circling, or the cap?"
> P: "Glad you went there, because the transparency is actually built into the agreement."
> C: "Gross revenue basis, fixed cap, defined term — those exist so there's zero ambiguity about where you stand. Cleanest version of this structure I could build."
> L: "Let me show you the milestone sequence so you can see exactly what the capital produces and when. Does that sound fair for the most part?"

## PHASE 4 — The Ask (the close)

You do not need a sneak attack. He already knows an ask is coming — you told him in Phase 0. So when the pitch has landed and he's done probing, you ask **clearly and warmly**, and you begin the paperwork as agreement forms. That's not an ambush; that's just closing.

**The transition into the close:**
> "So that's the whole thing. You've picked at it for an hour and it's still standing — which is exactly what I hoped, because I wanted *your* read specifically. Here's where I'm at: I'd want you in. Not as my dad doing me a favor — as someone whose judgment I trust putting capital into something he thinks will actually work. The question I've got for you is just *how much* and *in what shape* — lump sum or the monthly schedule."

That last line is the assumed close, done honestly: you've moved him from *if* to *how*, out loud, with his eyes open. With this man, that's the version that works.

**The genuine emotional anchors — these are TRUE, so say them as truth, not as triggers.** Drop them naturally where they fit; do not stack them or perform them.

- *(The strongest one — it's real and it's about him):* "You're the one who told me I could structure this however I wanted and still keep control of my own project. So I did. This whole structure exists because of something you taught me."
- *(Let him arrive at the family piece — don't spell it out):* "When this gets to the point of needing real IT and software infrastructure, I already know exactly who I'm calling." *(He'll fill in the name. Let him.)*
- *(Quiet, not dramatic):* "This is what it looks like when I'm finally doing the thing I'm actually built to do."

**Begin the paperwork as he engages:**
As the conversation turns to *how much* and *which schedule*, start filling in what you can — the same way the transcript describes ("what was your first name") — but because he already knows this is a real ask, this reads as momentum, not a trap. Keep it light: "Okay, let me start getting this down while we talk it through."

**Payment flexibility (lowers the size objection):**
> "And you don't need $30K on day one. It's $5K a month over six months if that's easier. You need five grand to start, not thirty."

**The smaller-tier fallback (keeps him in even at a no on the big number):**
> "And honestly, if the full thing isn't where you're at right now — five grand gets you in as a founding backer: named in everything I release, lifetime access to OCC, a goodwill share. That gets you on the ledger. We grow the rest as the company proves out."

**The "does that sound fair" close line, deployed for real:**
> "So — lump sum or monthly, and we get it started tonight. Does that sound fair for the most part?"

## PHASE 5 — If He Wants His Lawyer / Financial People First

**Do not route around this. Back it.** He's mid-divorce. A financial move made during active proceedings can be scrutinized, and the exposure is *his*. The honest play protects him and, with this audience, actually closes better — because pushing him to skip his own counsel would instantly read as the one thing he's trained to distrust.

> A *(pullback + question):* "That's exactly the right call — I'd be uncomfortable if you didn't. Is the hesitation the structure itself, or the timing with everything else you've got going on?"
> P: "Good — because the last thing I want is for this to create a headache for you on the other stuff."
> C: "This is a real arms-length revenue-share investment into a registered company. It documents cleanly and it holds up to your lawyer looking at it. I'd rather you come in clean and a little slower than fast and messy. Send them the agreement."
> L: "So take it, let your people look, and let's lock a firm time to talk again before I close the other slots. Does that sound fair for the most part?"

**Hard rule for you, not just for him:** do not build, suggest, or float any structure whose point is to keep this investment from being seen or understood by his spouse or her lawyers — including the out-of-province holding-company idea. That puts your father at real legal risk, and it's not a risk that's yours to take for him. If he invests, it's clean and disclosed or it doesn't happen. This isn't a tactical call; it's the floor.

## PHASE 6 — If It's a No (or a not-yet)

Protect the relationship above the deal. Always.

> "Totally fair — and I mean that. You looking at it this hard and being honest with me is worth a lot on its own. Two things: would you still be willing to be a set of eyes as I refine it for other investors? And — keep the door open. The slots are real, but if one's still there when your situation settles, I'd rather it be yours."

If he's out entirely on investment, that's fine — you've still gained the sharpest feedback you'll get anywhere, and you keep your father in your corner. That's not a loss. Walk out warm.
