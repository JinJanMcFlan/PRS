**Ringo — Terminal Games' IP (contract, NOT co-ownership):**

Any increase to Jonathan's share is a *contract renegotiation* between him and Andrew, entirely separate from any investment.

**Symmetry:** ProjectARC : Deuterium3D :: (Andrew's AI co) : Terminal Games. On each side the tech company funds the studio. Ringo is Terminal Games' project; Deuterium3D is a contracted creative partner on it.

## Legal status (current vs. future)

Right now the four "companies" are **trade names / operating identities, not separate legal entities.** They sit under two sole-proprietor business numbers:

- **Jonathan** — one BN, GST-registered. Operates ProjectARC + Deuterium3D. (Referred to as "employee" of both in plain terms; note that legally a sole-prop owner takes an owner's draw, not a salary — it becomes a real wage on incorporation.)
- **Andrew** — one BN, no GST registration. Operates (Andrew's AI co) + Terminal Games.

**The plan (both sides, same playbook):** incorporate as real entities once revenue justifies the cost and admin. ProjectARC incorporates; Deuterium3D stays a basic BN trade name linked to Jonathan. Andrew runs the mirror version on his side. So: think of the four as future incorporated companies that currently exist as named divisions under two individuals' sole props.

**Deal implication — what "gross" covers:** the revenue share is on Jonathan's *product revenue across his ventures* (ProjectARC tech + Deuterium3D creative, including Deuterium3D's Ringo cinematic/film payouts) — not just OCC. Rationale: anti-avoidance (revenue follows the business; he can't tank one name and run profit through another) and downside collateral for the investor ("if OCC fails, you still have access to everything Jonathan is building"). Part of why the cap is only 2× and why it's investor-favorable. Lawyer notes: (1) scope it to the business ecosystem (ProjectARC + divisions + successors/related ventures), NOT literally all of Jonathan's personal income, so it doesn't penalize unrelated work; (2) it excludes incoming investor capital — that is not revenue. Idle, un-deployed capital owes no return (not a loan, no interest on money that sits) — but the 5-year term clock keeps running, so sitting on capital just burns term.

## Cross-company help = work-based contribution (not equity)

When one side helps the other's product, it's handled as **contracted work, not a shared-equity tangle.** The helper is an expense to the product's owner; that company's investors get their return first, then the helper is paid as a line-item cost. Nothing more complicated.

- **Simple Stack** — a product of (Andrew's AI co), under development (NOT a joint product). If Jonathan contributes, it's work-based: (Name)'s investors are paid first, then Jonathan is an expense to (Name). Not a shared-IP / equity arrangement.
- **Same in reverse** — e.g. if Andrew stepped in to help produce OCC, that's just ProjectARC outsourcing work to another company. Contractor in, deliverable out.
- **Ringo (Terminal Games' game)** — during game development Jonathan is hired/outsourced by Terminal Games at going rates for tool development and potential tool marketing, and can license/charge for tools he builds for the game. The UE5 / Epic grant is the *game's* and goes to Terminal Games; Jonathan's upside is the paid work + tool licensing, which flow to his side as revenue (and count toward the investor's gross). No shared pot — just billed work.

(Ringo itself is not co-owned — it is Terminal Games' IP, with Deuterium3D holding contractual asset access + a payout cap. See the Ringo note up top.)
