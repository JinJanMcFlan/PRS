# ProjectARC — Investor Playbook
**Version:** 2.0 — Pending Founder Approval
**Date:** June 9, 2026
**Purpose:** Hard Q&A for every objection, probe, and skeptical question an investor raises. This document is memorized, not handed out. The answers here are the foundation the pitch scripts are built on.

**Primary audience profile:** Financial advisor, 35 years experience, mutual funds, sharp conceptual thinker, not AI or gaming literate, closer to film, going through a divorce, net worth ~$9M pre-divorce, actively doing his own deal work. Responds to professional structure, clear numbers, and a story he can repeat to others.

**Close technique in use:** APCL — Acknowledge, Pivot, Close, Loop.
- **A — Acknowledge:** "Yes, I understand that — I've actually heard that before from [reference]." Optionally open with a pullback to relieve pressure: "And look, you're not committing to anything right now..."
- **P — Pivot:** "I'm actually glad you brought that up — it was something I was thinking through when I set this up."
- **C — Close:** Hard close using an analogy or a reference to someone else who faced the same concern and moved past it. The analogy makes the objection feel resolved without debating it.
- **L — Loop:** Return to the pitch exactly where you left off. Continue like the objection never happened. Status stays high.

## SECTION 1 — The Deal Structure

**Q: Why revenue share and not equity?**
A: Equity means you have a say in how the company operates. Revenue share means you get paid when the company earns — full stop. You don't sit in on decisions about game design, product direction, or hiring. You're not a shareholder trying to protect a position. You put capital in, the company generates revenue, you receive your percentage until the cap is hit. Clean, simple, no governance overhead for either side.

**Q: Why gross revenue and not net?**
A: Net revenue is easy to manipulate — expenses can be loaded to reduce what looks like profit. Gross revenue is the top line. Every dollar that comes into ProjectARC counts toward your percentage before anything is subtracted. It protects the investor.

**Q: 4% of gross — how does that actually return $60,000 on a $30,000 investment?**
A: The cap is $60,000 — that's 2x the investment. 4% of gross revenue pays toward that cap until it's reached. At $750,000 in gross revenue, the cap is hit and the agreement closes. At $1.5M gross over 5 years — a conservative target for a studio with multiple revenue streams — the cap closes in the first half of the term. The investor gets out clean at 2x. The company keeps everything after that.

**Q: What if the company earns nothing in 5 years?**
A: Then the investor receives nothing and is owed nothing. This is not a loan. There is no repayment obligation. The investor's return is tied entirely to company performance. If the company fails, the capital is lost — the same as any early-stage investment. The difference is there is no equity dilution, no board seat, and no governance risk on either side.

**Q: What happens if revenue comes in slowly — does the term extend?**
A: No. The term is fixed at 5 years for Tranche 1. If the cap is not reached within 5 years, the agreement closes at the end of year 5 regardless of how much has been paid out. The investor receives whatever percentage has accumulated. The term protects the company from open-ended obligations.

**Q: Why 2x cap and not higher?**
A: 2x on a 5-year early-stage revenue share is a strong return. Comparable structures in early-stage creative and tech companies range from 1.5x to 3x depending on risk profile. 2x was chosen because it's defensible, fair, and clean. A higher cap would either require a lower percentage or a longer term — both of which reduce attractiveness. This structure is designed to close, not to negotiate.

**Q: Can I invest less than $30,000?**
A: Yes. The $5,000 minimum tier covers TRC production and studio launch. The $5,000–$15,000 range falls under the Project Sponsor structure — flat contribution, studio credits, first-look OCC access, and a 1–2% goodwill revenue share with a 1.5x cap. The Tier 1 Revenue Partner structure starts at $30,000.

**Q: Can I put in more than $30,000 but less than $200,000?**
A: Yes, and here's exactly how that works. The $200,000 roadmap has specific allocated targets:

| Allocation | Amount |
|---|---|
| Founder full-time (6 months living + debt elimination) | $30,000 |
| IT hire — software development, cybersecurity, infrastructure | ~$50,000 |
| Creative industry generalist — senior production leadership | ~$60,000–$80,000 |
| Hardware for new hires + studio infrastructure | ~$20,000–$30,000 |
| Operations buffer | Remainder |

The IT role is already identified — a SAIT-trained software developer and computer systems graduate with a cybersecurity background. The creative hire is someone with industry leadership experience that significantly exceeds the founder's — already watched, already in mind, circumstances being monitored.

If the amount invested falls between $30,000 and the threshold for a full hire, the excess over $30,000 is held in studio operations — it does not go to personal pay. It waits until it can fully fund a position. The investor knows exactly where every dollar is going, and has full right to refuse any specific allocation. If they won't fund a line item, it gets covered externally. This is not a negotiation — it's transparency.

**Q: What is the wage garnishment clause?**
A: It's a standard early-stage term. The agreement includes a 6-month deferral on any wage garnishment enforcement. Capital deployed into an early-stage operation returns faster when it stays working inside the business during the growth window rather than being diverted to enforcement costs. It is in the investor's interest for the capital to remain deployed and generating the revenue that pays the return.

**Q: Is this a formal legal agreement?**
A: Yes. The revenue share agreement is a binding legal document specifying the investment amount, revenue share percentage, cap, term, payment flexibility terms, and enforcement deferral clause. It is signed by both parties. Specific technical IP details are disclosed under NDA at the due diligence stage — standard practice for proprietary systems with no public equivalent.

*APCL trigger — "I want to see more detail on the numbers before I commit":*
- **A:** "Completely fair — and look, you're not committing to anything right now. A sharp eye on the numbers is exactly right."
- **P:** "I'm actually glad you want to go line by line — that was my intention when I put this together. Full transparency is a standard term in this agreement."
- **C:** "Most early-stage investors I've talked to have the same instinct. The ones who've moved forward are the ones who realized the structure answers the question before they finish asking it. The gross revenue basis, the fixed cap, the defined term — those exist specifically so there's no ambiguity about where you stand."
- **L:** "Anyway — let me walk you through the milestone sequence so you can see exactly what the capital produces and when..."

## SECTION 2 — The Business

**Q: Why should I believe ProjectARC can generate $750,000 in gross revenue in 5 years?**
A: Multiple revenue streams come online in sequence. OCC alone at $30/month with 500 paying users generates $180,000/year. 500 users is a conservative target for a product entering a market where comparable tools charge $30–$100/month and serve millions. The Terminal Games partnership adds a revenue share on Ringo sales. The social content channel adds platform revenue. The Technology Department adds vertical app revenue. The question is not whether the company can reach $750,000 — it is which stream gets there first.

**Q: What is OCC and why would someone pay for it?**
A: OCC is an AI orchestration platform. Right now, someone who uses AI professionally runs 5–10 different tools — image generation, 3D modelling, code, writing, video — and manually copies work between them, tracks their own API costs across platforms, and figures out which model handles which task best. That landscape changes constantly — new models release, existing models update, the best tool for a given job shifts month to month. OCC replaces all of that with a single interface that routes automatically, manages API budgets, knows the best tool for every job, and updates itself when the AI field shifts. For a heavy AI user it reduces operational costs by an estimated 20–40% while increasing output speed. The value proposition is immediate and quantifiable. The target market is already paying for multiple subscriptions — OCC consolidates and optimizes them.

**Q: What is HKB?**
A: HKB is the foundational orchestration system that OCC is built on. It is ProjectARC's proprietary AI management infrastructure — currently 75% complete. It coordinates all AI workflows, manages the asset pipeline, and will eventually power all consumer and enterprise products the Technology Department produces. It is not a product sold directly — it is the engine underneath everything that is sold.

**Q: What's the evidence that the AI asset pipeline actually works?**
A: The TRC submission is the evidence. The Tripo AI 3D Rendering Challenge is a global competition with professional judges evaluating render quality, craft, and production value. Deuterium3D is entering with assets produced entirely through the AI pipeline — no outsourced modeling, no purchased assets. If the entry places, that is independent third-party validation that the pipeline produces at competitive quality. That result feeds directly into the Epic MegaGrants application.

**Q: What is Epic MegaGrants and why does it matter?**
A: Epic MegaGrants is Epic Games' non-dilutive funding program for Unreal Engine 5 developers. 2026 Cycle 2 runs June 29 – September 4. Funding range: $5,000 to $150,000. No equity. No revenue recoupment. Full IP retention. It requires a working prototype, gameplay video, concept deck, and milestone budget — all of which will exist by submission. Target grant: maximum tier. This is non-dilutive capital that stacks on top of investor funding and costs the company nothing in return.

**Q: How far along is the game?**
A: First conceptual playtest completed with 5 external testers. One tester played 3 hours continuously in an unfinished greybox state because the movement system was that engaging — and in doing so developed emergent mechanics the team hadn't designed, which were documented and built into the architecture. 4 of 5 testers said unprompted they had never seen anything like it. One tester disengaged after 5 minutes because the difficulty was too high. That result was not a failure — it validated a known design gap and confirmed the next development priority: the game needs to teach movement through experiential level design, not explicit tutorials. Two distinct target markets are being served simultaneously — a high-skill movement market with significant word-of-mouth marketing potential, and a broad playerbase that buys in volume. The design philosophy addresses both without compromising either. Currently transitioning from conceptual phase into a 3-level working Alpha. End-of-year release target, contingent on quality and studio scale.

**Q: What happens if the game doesn't ship?**
A: The game is one revenue stream. OCC, the vertical apps, the social content channel, and the Technology Department products are independent of Ringo. If the game is delayed or cancelled, the other streams continue. The company's revenue model does not depend on the game shipping on schedule.

**Q: Who is Terminal Games and what is the relationship?**
A: Terminal Games is a separate company run by Andrew, the game's lead developer. Deuterium3D handles all cinematic, cutscene, and AI technology work for Ringo in exchange for a revenue share on game sales capped at approximately $100,000. They are collaborators on a shared deliverable — not co-founders, not the same company. Terminal Games is running its own funding round in parallel. The two studios operate under a structured partnership framework: each company owns its independently developed IP fully, and jointly developed technology is governed by tracked contribution agreements negotiated per project.

## SECTION 3 — The Founder

**Q: Why should I believe one person can build all of this?**
A: Because one person already has. In five months — outside of full-time employment, with no co-founder and no external development budget — the founder produced: tens of thousands of lines of functional code, multiple production-ready AI workflows, a foundational orchestration system at 75% completion, a near-complete AI asset pipeline, and a functional game in active playtesting. The output exists. It can be demonstrated. This pitch is not a plan for what will be built — it is a plan for what gets built next, by someone who has already proven they build.

**Q: What does full-time funding actually change?**
A: Right now the founder is working around a job. Full-time removes that constraint entirely. As a specific example: the TRC competition. Working part-time means 60–70 hour weeks split between a job and competition development — which is enough to place in the top 20. Full-time means 60 hours goes directly into the competition — which is enough to go for first place. That difference is what funding buys. Not the ability to work — the ability to work without a ceiling.

**Q: How do I know you'll actually work hard once you have the money?**
A: The five months before this pitch is the answer. There are no logged hours for that period — the output is the record. Going forward, all working hours are tracked by project for internal workflow optimization and productivity analysis. That system was already being built before this conversation. Investors have full visibility into that data as a standard transparency measure.

**Q: What's your background — why are you qualified to build this?**
A: Five years professional experience as a lighting artist. Two-year diploma covering the full 3D pipeline. Heavy experience in Maya and Nuke. Currently developing in Unreal Engine 5. The technical production background is complete. The AI systems knowledge was built through five months of intensive self-directed development — building tools, not just using them.

There's one other thing worth mentioning. The partner studio — Terminal Games — exists in part because of this company. Andrew was taught AI from the ground up by the founder. On multiple occasions Andrew has said directly that without that knowledge he would not have been able to put his dream into motion. That is Deuterium3D's stated mission made real: empower to create. It spawned Terminal Games' own motto: freedom to create. The proof of concept for the company's purpose was already running before the first investor conversation.

## SECTION 4 — The Risk

**Q: What are the biggest risks here?**
A: Three honest risks. First, timeline risk — the milestones are aggressive and depend on the founder staying healthy and focused. Second, market risk — OCC enters a competitive AI tools market. Third, game risk — Ringo's commercial performance is uncertain. Each has a specific mitigation — detailed below if probed.

**Q: What if a bigger company builds something like OCC first?**
A: Large companies are building general-purpose AI assistants. OCC is an orchestration layer — it routes between those tools and manages API costs across them. A larger company releasing a new AI tool does not obsolete OCC — it potentially adds a new tool OCC routes. The proprietary value is in the orchestration architecture and the self-updating field awareness, not in any single AI capability.

**Q: What if you can't get users for OCC?**
A: Phase 1 targets power AI users — people already paying for multiple AI subscriptions who understand API keys. This is a small, self-identifying market with immediate willingness to pay. The marketing is direct community presence in AI power-user spaces where the product demonstrates itself. The social content strategy builds this audience alongside the TRC campaign.

**Q: Is there any IP protection on the systems you've built?**
A: Proprietary systems are protected through trade secret and NDA at the disclosure stage. Certain technical architecture has no publicly documented equivalent. Formal IP strategy including patent assessment is part of the post-funding roadmap. Details are disclosed under NDA at due diligence. On the cybersecurity side — this is a known and acknowledged gap. The IT hire is specifically selected for cybersecurity expertise alongside software development and infrastructure management. Prior to that hire being in place, upon OCC's completion the founder moves directly into hardening the security architecture. Basic countermeasures are active. A full cybersecurity plan exists and is ready to execute. This is not an oversight — it is a sequenced priority.

**Q: What about your health — can you sustain this workload?**
A: Yes — and this is not an assumption, it is a managed system. The founder is currently training for a triathlon — not because it's enjoyable, but because a defined physical performance goal is the most effective tool for sustaining the cognitive and physical output this project requires. Sleep, nutrition, fitness, and blood work have all been researched and optimized for high-performance sustained output. High-performance counselling specialized in business owners and creatives is already in place — pre-funded independently. If the founder is not operating at full capacity, the project suffers. That is understood. The investment in personal performance is the mitigation.

## SECTION 5 — The Personal Close (Dad-Specific)

*These are not Q&A items — they are seed responses for objections that arise during the warm close. Use APCL structure throughout.*

**If he says "I need to run this past my lawyers / financial people":**

Do not treat this as an objection to overcome. Given that he is mid-divorce, this is the correct instinct and you want him to follow it. Trying to route around his lawyers here would expose *him* — a financial transaction made during active divorce proceedings can be scrutinized as concealment or dissipation of marital assets, and that risk is his, not yours. The honest, relationship-preserving, and frankly more persuasive move is to back his caution.

- **A (with question-pullback):** "That's exactly the right call — you should run it past them, and I'd be uncomfortable if you didn't. Quick question: is your hesitation about the structure itself, or about the timing given everything you've got going on right now?"
- **P:** "I'm glad you're treating it that way, because the last thing I want is for you to do something that creates a headache for you on the other stuff."
- **C:** "This is a genuine arms-length revenue-share investment into a registered company — not a personal loan, not a handshake. That means it can be documented cleanly and handled the right way with your own people in the loop. I'd rather you come in clean and slow than fast and messy. If your lawyer wants to look at the agreement, send it to them. It'll hold up."
- **L:** "So here's what I'd suggest — take the document, let your people look at it, and let's set a firm time to talk again before I close the other slots. Does that sound fair for the most part?"

**Do NOT use, ever:** any framing whose purpose is to keep the investment from being seen or understood by his spouse or her lawyers, and do not raise the out-of-province holding-company "identity" idea in this context. It puts your father at legal risk and it is not yours to take on his behalf. If he chooses to invest, clean and disclosed is the only version on the table.

**Reframe: "when and how, not if":**
You already understand this structure — you explained it to me. Revenue share, no equity, no control transfer, return tied to performance. You know what this is. You just spent two hours picking it apart and it held up. The question is not whether this is a sound investment. The question is when you want to be in and at what amount.

**The emotional close anchors — plant these, don't announce them:**
- "You told me I could structure this however I wanted and still keep control. So I did."
- "I know exactly who I'm calling when this company needs IT infrastructure and software development." *(let him arrive at who that is — don't say the name)*
- "This is what it looks like when I do what I'm supposed to be doing."
- "I'm not asking you to believe in a plan. I'm asking you to believe in what I've already built."

**If he says "I don't have that kind of liquid cash right now":**
- **A:** "That's completely fair — and again, you're not committing to the full amount right now."
- **P:** "I actually structured the payment specifically so this doesn't have to happen all at once."
- **C:** "The flexibility is $5,000/month over 6 months. You don't need $30,000 on day one — you need $5,000 on day one. And if $30,000 is still too large, the $5,000 minimum puts you in as a Project Sponsor — named in every studio release, lifetime OCC access, and a goodwill revenue share. That gets you in. We figure out the rest as the company performs."
- **L:** "Anyway — let me show you where to sign and we can talk through the payment schedule..."

**Budget transparency offer (deploy during close if he probes allocations):**
Full line-item visibility is a standard offer. If there's a specific allocation he won't fund — whether that's a software subscription, a personal expense, anything — it gets covered externally. This is not a negotiation. It is transparency. The investor holds the right to refuse any specific use of their capital. That right is built into the agreement.
