## What this website actually is

It's a **business card**, not a portfolio or brochure — a business card that walks someone through the whole business structure. You land on it, and by the time you hit the bottom you understand the entire thing: what we make, what powers it, and the company underneath that built the power.

**One site doing two jobs on purpose.** Budget and time for one site only, so it has to work for two completely different people at once. It does *not* split into two — it's the **same page read at two different depths.**

---

## Structure — top to bottom

The page reads top to bottom, and the read tells a story in three beats:

**made it → empowered it → built it**

- **Top — Deuterium3D.** The work. The art. *This is what we made.*
- **Middle — the bridge.** How the work actually gets made. *This is what empowered it.*
- **Bottom — ProjectARC.** The backend AI tools. *This is how it's built.*

Those three are the section labels going down the page.

**Brand lockup placement (LOCKED):** The line "Deuterium3D powered by ProjectARC" does **not** go in the headline. It lives in the **pinned top bar (Deuterium3D · ARC · Ringo)** as the lockup — always present, quietly. When you scroll into each section, that tab **expands into the full name and swaps to that entity's description.**

---

## The real strategy — two audiences, one page

- **Surface read:** a front for regular people hopping off X / YouTube to peek at something that looks sick. Marketing job, social hook.
- **Underneath:** a meticulously structured document showcasing our strongest points to investors as they work down the page, until they basically *need* to get involved with ARC.

Same page, two depths. Visuals pull everyone in; structure converts the ones who matter. **No separate investor track, no hidden investor page** — the page itself *is* the proof. Hand it to an investor and the context lands on its own: they realize they're looking at the actual product made by the company they'd be investing in.

---

## First impression — and the open loop

Three beats, in order:

1. **Gut punch** — "woah, that's sick, this is so cool." The wow is aimed at *the work*, not the website. **The site design supports and highlights the work — it never upstages it.** The work is the star.
2. **Understanding** — they get that Deuterium3D makes **cutscenes and animated film, not games.** Same lane as Goldtooth — game cinematics and animated story content, not the games themselves.
3. **The open loop** — they leave the top of the page thinking *"how do they do this so fast and at this quality?"* That question is deliberate. It's the hook that drags them into the bridge and the ARC reveal where the "how" finally gets answered. A regular viewer feels it as curiosity; an investor feels it as "what's the engine here?" **Same pull, both end up at ARC.**

---

## Headline, lockup, proof

- **Headline:** *"Worlds worth watching."* Work-first. No AI in it, no brand structure in it — the top bar carries the brand; the bottom of the page reveals the tools.
- **Subline:** about the work, with life to it (e.g. *"the cutscenes you don't skip — and the films you don't forget."*). Never a dry museum placard. Never the AI pipeline. The AI gets *teased* by the proof line, not explained up top.
- **Proof line:** short and blunt near the video (*"Full teaser. 3 weeks."*). A longer, more exciting version lives in the D3D about tab. **Opens the loop without answering it.**

---

## The video

A **full-bleed background video** sitting behind the headline. Currently a placeholder — nothing finished yet. Swap path: short → animatic → real cuts → eventually a montage of best work, **Marvel-opening style.**

**Hard rule: it cannot look like a video box.** It's integrated into the site.

**Fullscreen behavior:** click → goes fullscreen, but NOT the way every other video does. **The rest of the site animates out of the way and off screen as the player expands, and animates back when you close it.** Special, professional, not goofy. Exact mechanic is flexible — it just has to feel special.

## The ARC reveal + scarcity mechanic

The ARC reveal is **medium** — but only in the context that works directly for D3D. ARC explains how its overly adaptive tools were used to build a specialized workflow for Deuterium3D, getting into the gritty of how that pipeline replaced entire departments so one person could make this project instead of a whole studio.

**It's a sales pitch through an effective use case.**

**Scarcity rule (LOCKED):** Every piece of actually usable info stays **locked behind access to the tool.** Show the *what* and the *result* in full. Withhold the *how*.

**Tool names (OCC, HKB, ORG, TRC):** placeholders for now, explained at a later date. When explained, that section should be an **artistic mind-map / flowchart**, in the spirit of the 4-box numbered flow near the middle-bottom of the original mockups — elevated into something genuinely crafted. Real project flowcharts will be supplied as source material.

## Contact, socials, and what we want people to do

- **ARC: contact direct, no socials — ever.** Private company. You reach it directly.
- **D3D and Ringo have socials.** The follow buttons live **with their sections**, not as one catch-all button at the bottom (down there nobody knows if they're following D3D, ARC, or Ringo).
- **Main action:** casual visitors watch the work and follow D3D / Ringo. The ones who matter contact ARC to learn more. **No waitlist, no fake scarcity buttons.**

**Phase 2:** pour in real assets as we produce them, plus the deeper ARC reveal. **Build the vessel now; fill it as we go.**

## How it should FEEL

Like a **conversation**, not a form or a brochure. High-class, clear, intelligent — should treat the visitor like they're being walked from the private jet to the yacht, even if they're a run-of-the-mill guy. **Confident, never desperate.** We don't pitch on the glass — the page earns the conversation by being good, then the ask shows up late, after the work has already sold them.

---

## Why it's set up this way

It's a **pitch arc turned into a page.** Same rule as any good pitch: rapport before the ask. Establish the work and the business first, reveal the engine last, and let the ask — *get involved with ARC* — show up near the bottom once they're already sold by what they've seen.

**The art is the proof that the tools work.** The whole page exists to take someone from *"that's sick"* → *"how?"* → *"I need in."*
